Genetic factors: Many different genes give you the framework to learn and use language. They affect things like brain function and muscle control.
Language is a trait that is particularly subject to influence during certain stages of life. Even as we learn to speak one language, we start to lose the ability to make sounds that are used in other languages. Adults tend to be slower than children to pick up a new language. And for most of us, it’s very hard to speak a new language accent-free if we learn it after puberty.
Babies are born with the potential to learn any language. Which specific language(s) they learn is completely determined by what’s spoken around them.
The attitudes and behaviors of a person’s friends and family toward smoking, for example, can play a large part in whether he or she will develop an addiction.
Genetic factors: Many genes affect how a person’s brain works, influencing their susceptibility to addiction.

Asthma
For some people, strenuous exercise can cause asthma symptoms.
Genetic factors: It’s estimated that about half a person’s risk for asthma comes from variation in genes, while the other half is from the environment.
Air Quality
It’s important to remember environmental factors don’t always have a negative effect. In the case of asthma, living in a rural area, having a childhood pet, or growing up in a large family may actually help. Growing up in these scenarios exposes people to a wide variety of microbial organisms, which helps them build a resistance to asthma. Scientists are still working to understand the details of how this works.
Developmental environment
Often people don’t experience asthma symptoms until they encounter pollution or allergens. Even something like baking flour that seems harmless can sometimes trigger asthma.
Sun exposure
Genetic factors: Variations in a handful of genes can influence your risk for skin cancer. One example is a version of the gene MC1R, which gives people red hair and fair skin.
At high elevations, the atmosphere provides less protection from the sun. Because of this, people who live at high altitudes have a greater risk for skin cancer.
Repeated sun exposure increases a person’s risk for skin cancer, because the sun can damage DNA in skin cells.

Vitamin D levels
People living near the poles experience less direct sunlight, so they’re more likely to be deficient in vitamin D. In places with seasons, people are at a higher risk or vitamin D deficiency in the winter than in the summer.
While vitamin D is often called the sunshine vitamin, you can get it from your diet as well. Eating fish, drinking fortified milk and orange juice, or taking a supplement can help you meet recommended levels.
Spending some time in the sun can be good for you. It causes you to produce vitamin D, which is important for the health of your bones and immune system.
Genetic factors: Certain genes (and the proteins they code for) are needed to convert vitamin D into an active form your body can use, and to transport it to where it’s needed. Variations in these genes can cause people to have more or less available vitamin D.

Nutrition
Imagine you have two corn plants with identical genetic material, but plant one in nutrient-rich soil and one in sand. Even though the two plants have the same genetics, their traits are likely to be very different.
The amount of sunlight a plant gets has a huge impact on its growth. With too little light, plants become pale and spindly. With too much, they can become stunted and sun damaged, and even die. For some plants, changes in the number of daylight hours causes them to grow flowers or go dormant.
Genetic factors: Many genes work together during plant growth. These genes code for proteins that build tissue, control plant growth hormones, and carry out other important jobs.
